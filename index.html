Out There

Scientists Reveal Near-Earth Asteroid Aphophis More Likely to Strike Than Previously Believed

Here's what you need to know.

By Jeremy Chea

Nov. 9, 2024

In a stunning new development, NASA scientists have found that the threat posed by Potentially Hazardous Asteroids (PHAs) now commonly referred to as near-Earth asteroids (NEAs) is more significant than previously believed.

Recent research on asteroid Apophis, one of the most closely monitored NEAs, revealed an increased likelihood of impact with Earth, with odds shifting from 1 in 2 billion to 1 in 1 million (Phys.org, 2024). This significant update has reignited concerns about the real dangers of NEAs and prompted renewed focus on tracking and mitigating these potential threats.

Near-Earth asteroids, or NEAs, are rocky bodies that orbit the Sun and occasionally come close to Earths orbit. Unlike distant asteroids in the main asteroid belt, NEAs have paths that bring them within a specific range of Earth, classifying them as objects of interest for planetary defense. These asteroids vary in size, composition, and orbit, with some as small as a few meters and others stretching over a kilometer. What sets NEAs apart is not only their proximity to Earth but also the potential changes in their orbits due to gravitational interactions with planets, especially with Earth itself.

While the probability of a catastrophic impact remains low on a yearly basis, the stakes are high if even one large NEA were to collide with Earth. History provides stark examples of the potential consequences. The Chicxulub impact, which likely led to the extinction of the dinosaurs, was caused by an asteroid estimated to be over 10 kilometers in diameter. More recent events, like the 2013 Chelyabinsk meteor explosion over Russia, remind us that even smaller NEAs can cause significant damage, producing shockwaves that injure thousands and destroy infrastructure. The renewed findings about Apophis underscore the importance of monitoring these objects, as their unpredictable orbits could one day bring them closer than anticipated.

NASA and other space agencies use a combination of radar, telescopes, and specialized software to detect and track NEAs. Ground-based telescopes and space-based observatories constantly scan the sky for new objects, and when one is identified, radar systems provide detailed measurements of its size, shape, rotation, and orbit. NASAs Center for Near-Earth Object Studies (CNEOS) plays a pivotal role in cataloging NEAs, calculating their trajectories, and assessing the likelihood of potential impacts. These predictions help scientists understand the probability of future close approaches and the need for possible mitigation efforts.

In response to the potential threat of NEAs, NASA has developed strategies for planetary defense, including the Double Asteroid Redirection Test (DART) mission. DART, launched in 2021, was a groundbreaking experiment designed to test whether a kinetic impact could alter the orbit of an asteroid. In 2022, DART successfully collided with the asteroid Dimorphos, demonstrating for the first time that humans have the capability to adjust an asteroids path. This mission represents a significant step forward in planetary defense and offers a viable strategy should an NEA be predicted to threaten Earth in the future.

Looking forward, NASA and other space agencies are focused on enhancing our capabilities to detect, track, and, if necessary, deflect potentially hazardous asteroids. Future missions may involve more advanced impactors, gravity tractors, or even nuclear deflection techniques, depending on the size and speed of the target asteroid. The updated data on Apophis serves as a reminder that our efforts to monitor and prepare for NEAs must continue to evolve. With new technologies and international collaboration, scientists aim to ensure Earth remains protected against the real dangers that NEAs, like Apophis, may one day present.
